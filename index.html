A Pokemon card can be so much expensive than you think. Who is buying a $543,000 card?

By Haiyi Bi

Back in the days, Pokemon cards were among kids who just buy them for fun. In the late 1990s, a pack of five Pokemon cards cost about four dollars. Now, trading cards became a fashionable investment, just like how Labubu became a cultural phenomenon.

The market culture has been growing rapidly in North America and Asia, and it is not only a nostalgia collection purpose. People buy, trade, sell, or even hold in-person events around Pokemon cards. Keiji Umehara, a Youtuber creating content of Pokemon cards in Singapore, said that it became both a hobby and a business.

"Personally, I have been a collector since I was a kid. I like Pokémon, and I collect mainly because I enjoy it. I buy cards, but I do not sell very much," he said.

So what actually drives the price? A few things. First, the grading. PSA, the biggest card grading company, scores cards on a 1 to 10 scale. A PSA 10, meaning the card is in virtually perfect condition, can be worth 10 or even 50 times more than the same card at a PSA 8. For old cards printed in the late '90s, finding one in perfect shape is genuinely rare.

Then there's the print run. First Edition cards from the original Base Set were only printed once. When the Pokemon Company realized how much demand there was, they reprinted the set without the 1st Edition stamp. That small difference on the card now means a difference of tens of thousands of dollars.

And finally, there's the cultural factor. Pokemon didn't just stay popular with kids. The people who collected cards in the late '90s grew up, got jobs, and started buying back the cards they had as children. When Logan Paul wore a $150,000 Charizard around his neck in 2020, it kicked off a speculation frenzy that pushed prices even higher.

The market has cooled off since then, but the data still tells the story: a handful of species, a handful of rare prints, and a lot of people who never quite got over their childhood obsession.
